# Audio & Recording Setup Guide

> For screen-only content creators. Voice is everything — this guide makes sure it sounds great.

---

## The Reality: Audio > Video

For screen-only content, your audio quality determines your audience retention more than anything else.

**Bad audio = instant scroll.** You could have the best script in the world, but if the first 2 seconds sound muddy, muffled, or have background noise, viewers swipe away before hearing a single word.

Good news: you don't need expensive gear. You need the right cheap gear and to avoid common mistakes.

---

## Equipment (Budget: Rp 300k - 1.5 juta)

### Tier 1: Absolute Minimum (Rp 300k)

**Boya BY-M1 lavalier mic** — Rp 100-150k
- Plugs into phone or laptop via 3.5mm jack
- Clip to your shirt
- 10x better than phone built-in mic
- Works with Android + iPhone (with adapter)

**Over-ear headphones** (whatever you have)
- Used for monitoring while you record
- Catches audio problems before they're in the final video
- Any decent headphones work

**Quiet room** (free)
- Record in a room with soft surfaces (bed, sofa, curtains)
- Close windows
- Turn off AC if possible
- Record after 10 PM or before 7 AM for traffic-free audio

**Cost: Rp 100-150k**

### Tier 2: Better Quality (Rp 500k - 1 juta)

**Rode Wireless ME** (Rp 1-1.5 juta) OR **Hollyland Lark M1** (Rp 800k-1 juta)
- Wireless lavalier system
- Transmitter clips to you, receiver plugs into phone/laptop
- Pro-quality audio
- Built-in noise reduction
- Works up to 100m range (doesn't matter for you, but nice)

**Treatment: Egg crate foam** (Rp 100-200k for a small panel)
- Tape to wall behind your recording spot
- Kills echo
- Makes you sound like you're in a studio

**Cost: Rp 900k - 1.7 juta**

### Tier 3: Studio Quality (Rp 2-5 juta)

**Shure MV7+ USB microphone** (Rp 3-4 juta)
- Podcast-grade USB mic
- Plugs directly into laptop
- Built-in processing
- What most serious podcasters use

**Mic arm/boom** (Rp 200-500k)
- Keeps mic at perfect distance
- Hands-free
- Doesn't pick up desk vibrations

**Acoustic panels** (Rp 300-500k for set of 6)
- Proper studio treatment
- Ideal for long-form YouTube content
- Overkill for TikTok shorts (but future-proof)

**Cost: Rp 3.5 - 5 juta**

### My recommendation for you:

**Start with Tier 1 (Rp 150k).** Get the Boya BY-M1 or similar cheap lavalier. Record your first 5 videos with it. See if you actually enjoy making content and get any traction.

**Upgrade to Tier 2 (Rp 1 juta) only if:**
- You've posted at least 10 videos
- You're getting some engagement
- You're committed to 90 days
- Your current audio is the bottleneck

**Skip Tier 3** unless you're making long-form YouTube videos AND you're making money from content.

## Software Setup

### Screen recording

**QuickTime (Mac)** — free, built-in
- File → New Screen Recording
- Select microphone input (set to your lav mic)
- Good enough for short videos

**OBS Studio (Mac/Windows/Linux)** — free
- More control, more features
- Scene composition, multiple sources
- Learning curve is 1 hour
- Better for longer content

**ScreenFlow (Mac)** — Rp 1.5 juta one-time
- Professional screen recording + editing in one app
- What many creators use
- Worth it if you do lots of editing

**Loom** — free tier available
- Cloud-based
- Fast, shareable
- Not ideal for editing
- Good for quick drafts

**Recommended: QuickTime for Mac, OBS for Windows.**

### Video editing

**CapCut** — free, best for short-form
- Mobile app (record AND edit on phone)
- AI captions (auto-generate, very accurate for English)
- Free music library
- Templates for TikTok/Reels format
- **This is what you should use for 90% of content**

**DaVinci Resolve** — free, desktop only
- Professional-grade editor
- Overkill for shorts, perfect for long-form YouTube
- Steep learning curve
- Free version has everything you need

**Descript** — paid, unique approach
- Edits video by editing the transcript (text)
- AI features built in
- $15/mo — worth it if you do lots of voice content
- Best for voice-only creators

**Recommended: CapCut for shorts, DaVinci for YouTube long-form (when you start).**

### Caption generation

**CapCut auto-captions** — free
- Works well for clear English speech
- Some errors on technical terms (Claude, Wokku, etc.)
- Always review and fix before publishing

**Submagic** — paid, Rp 150-300k/mo
- Better for viral-style captions
- Word-by-word highlights
- Style templates
- Not necessary, but makes captions more eye-catching

**Recommended: CapCut for free, upgrade to Submagic only if captions become a bottleneck.**

## Voice Technique for Non-Native English Speakers

Your accent is fine. Here's what actually matters for English audio content:

### 1. Speak slower than feels natural

You'll feel like you're talking too slowly. You're not — it sounds normal to listeners.

Why: native English speakers talk fast because their brain processes the language automatically. You're thinking about words, so your natural pace is already faster than optimal for listeners who are multitasking.

**Target: 130-150 words per minute.** Count words in a script, divide by 60, that's your target pace.

### 2. Emphasize one word per sentence

Native English has melodic emphasis. Each sentence usually has one stressed word.

**Example:**
- Monotone: "I've been a developer for fifteen years."
- Emphasized: "I've been a developer for *fifteen* years."

Mark your scripts before recording. Underline the word you'll stress. It sounds 10x more engaging.

### 3. Use pauses intentionally

Silence is a tool. Use it after important sentences.

**Bad:** "I've been coding for fifteen years and now I'm building products with AI and it's really exciting."

**Good:** "I've been coding for fifteen years. *[pause]* Now I'm building products with AI. *[pause]* It's really exciting."

Pauses give viewers time to absorb. They also make you sound confident, not nervous.

### 4. Don't fake an American accent

Your Indonesian-English accent is a feature, not a bug. Faking an American accent sounds worse than owning your natural one.

What to do:
- Pronounce words clearly (not "correctly" by American standards)
- If a word is hard for you, use a different word
- Own your accent with confidence

Examples of non-native accents that work on global YouTube:
- Theo t3.gg (American but fast/imperfect)
- Traversy Media (American, laid back)
- Fireship (Canadian, precise)
- Many Indian dev YouTubers with strong accents and millions of subs
- Many European creators — French, German, Eastern European accents

Your accent doesn't limit reach. Unclear audio does.

### 5. Record a test sentence before each session

Say: "Testing, testing. This is a test of my audio setup. One, two, three, four, five."

Play it back. Ask yourself:
- Is the volume loud and clear?
- Is there any background noise?
- Is there echo?
- Do I sound "boxy" or muddy?

Fix any issues before recording the actual video. It's faster to diagnose for 2 minutes than re-record a 60-second video.

## Common Audio Mistakes (Avoid These)

### Mistake 1: Built-in phone mic
Your phone's mic is designed for phone calls, not recording content. It picks up way too much ambient noise and sounds tinny.

**Fix:** Use a lavalier mic. Even Rp 100k is massively better.

### Mistake 2: Recording in a big, empty room
Empty rooms have echo. Hard walls + hard floors = boomy, unprofessional sound.

**Fix:** Record in a smaller room with soft surfaces (bed, sofa, curtains, clothes closet). Yes, many YouTubers literally record in their closet.

### Mistake 3: Mic too far from mouth
Distant mic = thin, distant voice. Sounds unprofessional.

**Fix:** Lavalier should be 6-8 inches (15-20 cm) from your mouth. Clip to your shirt or shirt collar.

### Mistake 4: Recording with windows open
Traffic, birds, neighbors, wind — all end up in your audio.

**Fix:** Close windows. Record at quiet times (late night, early morning). Turn off fans and AC.

### Mistake 5: Not monitoring with headphones
You don't hear problems until after editing.

**Fix:** Wear headphones during recording. Plug them into your recording device. Hear exactly what the mic hears.

### Mistake 6: Clipping (audio peaks)
If your voice is too loud, it distorts. Very hard to fix in editing.

**Fix:** Check audio levels. Peak should be around -12 dB to -6 dB. Never hit 0 dB (red zone).

### Mistake 7: Inconsistent volume across videos
Some videos loud, some quiet. Viewers have to adjust volume every time.

**Fix:** Use CapCut's "normalize" feature. Or target -16 LUFS (industry standard for short-form).

## Recording Workflow (repeatable)

### Before each recording session:

1. **Clean workspace**
   - Close unrelated browser tabs
   - Hide dock/taskbar notifications
   - Clean desktop
   - Turn off phone notifications on laptop

2. **Audio check**
   - Plug in mic
   - Put on headphones
   - Record 10-second test
   - Playback and verify

3. **Prepare visuals**
   - Open all browser tabs/editor files you'll show
   - Arrange them so cmd-tab works smoothly
   - Practice the flow once without recording

4. **Environment**
   - Close door
   - Silence phone
   - Tell people not to disturb you for 30 minutes
   - Drink water (dry mouth kills audio quality)

### During recording:

1. **Start with 3 seconds of silence** (easier to edit beginning)
2. **Say the script clearly** — full stops, pauses, emphasis
3. **If you mess up**, pause 2 seconds and redo that sentence (don't start over)
4. **End with 3 seconds of silence** (easier to edit ending)

### After recording:

1. **Review once** — check for unfixable issues (mic glitch, barking dog)
2. **Transfer to editor** immediately
3. **Edit within 24 hours** (fresh ears catch more)
4. **Export at high quality** (don't compress twice)

## Your First Week: Concrete Setup

### Day 1 (shopping/ordering)
- [ ] Order Boya BY-M1 lavalier mic (Tokopedia/Shopee)
- [ ] Download CapCut on phone
- [ ] Download OBS (if using desktop) or check QuickTime works
- [ ] Identify quiet room in your house
- [ ] Buy water bottle (dry mouth will kill your sessions)

### Day 2-3 (setup/testing)
- [ ] Mic arrives, test with phone and laptop
- [ ] Record 30-second test — say the alphabet
- [ ] Play back with headphones
- [ ] Adjust mic position until it sounds natural
- [ ] Test in different rooms, pick the quietest

### Day 4 (first practice recording)
- [ ] Read Script #1 out loud 3 times without recording
- [ ] Record it 3 times in a row (don't obsess over quality)
- [ ] Pick the best take
- [ ] Edit in CapCut
- [ ] **Don't post yet** — you're practicing

### Day 5-6 (second practice)
- [ ] Record the "First Video" script
- [ ] Review, refine
- [ ] Record 2-3 takes until you like one
- [ ] Edit with captions
- [ ] Still don't post — review it fresh in the morning

### Day 7 (post!)
- [ ] Watch yesterday's recording
- [ ] If it's okay (not perfect — okay), post it
- [ ] Post to TikTok first
- [ ] Don't check metrics for 24 hours
- [ ] Start Script #1 for next posting day

The first week is about getting comfortable with the process, not making viral content. Just post.

## Troubleshooting

### "My voice sounds muddy/muffled"
- Mic too far from mouth → move closer
- Recording in too big a room → add soft surfaces
- Post-processing too heavy → turn off noise reduction

### "There's a hissing/hum in background"
- Electrical interference → move away from power cables/routers
- Mic gain too high → lower input volume
- Room noise → find quieter room, record at different time

### "I sound nervous/unsure"
- Not enough takes → record 3-5 takes, pick best
- Reading directly → memorize key phrases, improvise around
- Script too long → cut to essentials
- Nervous energy → take 3 deep breaths before recording

### "My English pronunciation is bad on certain words"
- Replace the word with a synonym
- Slow down specifically on that word
- Over time, you'll improve — don't wait to be perfect
- Viewers don't care as much as you think

### "I hate hearing my own voice"
- Everyone hates their recorded voice at first
- This passes after 5-10 videos
- Listen to your voice daily — normal exposure desensitizes
- Focus on whether the content is valuable, not how you sound

## Final Note

**Perfect audio is overrated. Good enough audio is essential.**

Spend 1 hour getting your setup right. Then stop tweaking and start recording. The 11th video will sound better than the 1st regardless of gear — because you'll be better at speaking, not because the gear changed.

The gear is the floor. Your skill is the ceiling. Invest in both, but prioritize recording over gear.
