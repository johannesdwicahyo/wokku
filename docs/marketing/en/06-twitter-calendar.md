# Twitter/X Content Calendar — First 30 Days

> Twitter/X is your secondary channel. Lower effort than video, higher value for networking and credibility. Aim for 3-5 tweets per week, plus 1 thread.

---

## Twitter Strategy

### Your positioning on Twitter

**Bio-aligned**: Senior dev (15y) exploring agentic engineering, building indie products.

**What you post:**
1. Short observations ("I noticed X about Y")
2. Tips ("Here's a prompt that saved me X minutes")
3. Building-in-public updates ("Shipped Y today")
4. Quote-tweets with your take
5. Replies to bigger accounts (visibility play)
6. Occasional threads (deeper dives)

**What you DON'T post:**
- Generic motivational content
- Hot takes designed to rage-bait
- Political commentary
- Self-promotional spam
- "Good morning Twitter"

### Posting frequency

**Sustainable target:**
- 3-5 short tweets per week
- 1 thread per week
- 10-20 replies per week to other accounts
- 5 quote-tweets per week

Not aiming for viral. Aiming for **consistent signal**.

## Twitter Thread Template

Once a week, post a thread. Here's the structure:

### Thread format (5-8 tweets)

**Tweet 1 (the hook):**
- Bold claim or curious question
- Sets up the rest
- Must earn the click to "Show this thread"

**Tweets 2-6 (the meat):**
- One point per tweet
- Screenshots where possible
- Numbered if you're counting things
- Each tweet readable as standalone

**Tweet 7-8 (the close):**
- Summary or lesson
- Call to action (follow, try the product, reply)
- Link to longer content (video, blog) if applicable

### Example thread: "5 prompts I use every day"

**Tweet 1:**
```
After using Claude Code for 6 months as a solo founder, I have 5 prompts I type every single day.

Not "here's how to write Hello World." Actual prompts that saved me dozens of hours this year.

🧵
```

**Tweet 2:**
```
1/ Context prompt:

"Read [file1.rb] and [file2.rb], then summarize the pattern used for [feature]. I want to apply the same pattern to [new thing]."

Teaches Claude your codebase conventions before it writes any code.
```

**Tweet 3:**
```
2/ Constraint prompt:

"Implement X. Don't add new dependencies. Follow the pattern in [existing file]. Keep it under 100 lines."

Constraints > creativity. Most bad AI code comes from AI being too creative.
```

**Tweet 4:**
```
3/ Test-first prompt:

"Write failing tests for [feature]. Don't implement yet. I'll review the tests first."

Tests become your spec. Claude can't accidentally build the wrong thing.
```

**Tweet 5:**
```
4/ Refactor prompt:

"Refactor [file] into smaller, focused classes. Don't change behavior. All existing tests must still pass."

The 'don't change behavior' part is critical. Claude likes to 'improve' things. Block that.
```

**Tweet 6:**
```
5/ Review prompt:

"Here's my diff. Before I commit, check for: unused code, security issues, unnecessary complexity, and deviations from the existing style."

Claude as second pair of eyes on my own code. Catches things I miss.
```

**Tweet 7:**
```
These 5 cover maybe 80% of my daily Claude Code usage.

None of them are clever. All of them are specific.

The pattern: tell Claude WHAT, WHERE, and CONSTRAINTS. Let it handle the HOW.
```

**Tweet 8:**
```
Full walkthrough of each prompt (with examples from my Wokku codebase) in the pinned video.

If you're using Claude Code daily, the techniques compound fast.
```

## Engagement Strategy

### Who to reply to

**Tier 1 (daily, whenever they post):**
- Developers whose content aligns with your pillars
- Indie hackers in your space
- Anthropic/Claude official accounts (relevant to your content)

**Tier 2 (weekly):**
- Bigger accounts (100k+) in dev Twitter
- Add thoughtful context to their posts
- Visibility plays — your replies can get more views than your original posts

**Tier 3 (occasionally):**
- Trending tech debates
- Controversial takes where you have experience
- New tool releases

### Reply philosophy

**Good replies:**
- Add your experience ("I've seen this, here's what worked")
- Ask clarifying questions
- Respectfully disagree with data
- Share your own take with evidence

**Bad replies:**
- "Great post!"
- "Agreed 💯"
- Random tag-alongs
- Self-promotion

### Reply template for building engagement

When someone in your space posts:
```
[Genuine reaction to their specific point]

[Your related experience or perspective]

[Soft question or invitation to continue]
```

**Example:**

Their post: "Spent 4 hours debugging a race condition today. Multi-threading is hard."

Your reply:
```
Race conditions are the worst because they don't show up in your dev environment.

I had a similar issue in Wokku last month — took me 3 hours to realize it only happened under specific job ordering.

What ended up being the fix for yours?
```

Builds: credibility + relevance + relationship.

## Twitter Red Flags

### Stop doing these if you catch yourself:

1. **Obsessing over metrics** — Check once a day max
2. **Writing engagement-bait** — "What do you think?" on every tweet
3. **Copying viral tweet formats** — Your voice is more valuable
4. **Getting into arguments** — Disagree once, then let it go
5. **Tweeting when tired/angry** — Schedule drafts, review when fresh
6. **Comparing yourself to accounts with 100k+ followers** — Different game
7. **Posting the same link 5 times** — Stop, nobody is impressed

### Signs you're doing it right:

1. **Conversations starting** — People reply and you reply back
2. **DMs from real people** — Questions, not spam
3. **Retweets from smaller accounts** — They resonate with your content
4. **Repeat engagers** — Same people showing up in replies
5. **Organic growth** — 10-50 new followers per week, steadily
6. **Drive to Wokku** — People clicking through to your product

Not: follower count, likes, vanity metrics.

## Final Twitter Note

Twitter is a slow burn. You won't see results in week 1. You might not see them in month 1.

But by month 3, the same people start engaging repeatedly. By month 6, strangers recognize your name. By month 12, you have a small but loyal network of developers who actually care what you post.

That's the real prize. Not followers — relationships.

Build relationships with your consistent, honest posting. Everything else follows.
